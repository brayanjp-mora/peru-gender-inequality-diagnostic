### A Survey-Based Analysis of Gender Inequality in Peru: A Purchasing Power Perspective

**Overview**

This project provides a quantitative diagnostic of gender-based economic inequality in Peru using nationally representative household survey data from 2017 to 2024. Rather than focusing solely on average gender gaps, the analysis examines how purchasing power varies across population groups defined by socioeconomic, demographic, and geographic characteristics.

The study is guided by the United Nations’ *Leave No One Behind (LNOB)* principle, which emphasizes the identification of population groups that are systematically disadvantaged. In this project, LNOB dimensions—such as discrimination, geography, vulnerability, socioeconomic status, and governance—are explored through a purchasing power lens, focusing on how economic outcomes differ across groups.

The objective is to support evidence-informed, equity-oriented policy analysis by identifying subgroups that are consistently positioned at lower levels of purchasing power and experience relatively larger gender disparities. This work is intended as a policy-oriented diagnostic and analytical foundation for future research, intervention design, and theory-of-change development.

---

**Research Objectives**

This project aims to:
	•	Measure gender-based disparities in purchasing power over time
	•	Identify population subgroups that experience relatively lower purchasing power and larger gender gaps
	•	Examine how economic inequality varies across regions and social characteristics
	•	Contribute to inclusive, rights-based policy analysis
	•	Provide a transparent and adaptable analytical framework for future work
	
---

**Methodology (Summary)**

The analysis applies a quantitative framework combining descriptive statistics and subgroup identification techniques to examine disparities in purchasing power.

Subgroups are identified based on two key criteria: (i) low median purchasing power levels and (ii) relatively larger gender gaps compared to a reference group. This approach is descriptive and does not imply causal relationships, but highlights patterns of inequality across population groups.

The analysis adopts a conservative “safe-fail” framework designed to prioritize estimate stability, interpretability, and ethical restraint over maximal disaggregation. Subgroup estimates are retained only if they meet minimum thresholds for sample size, statistical reliability, and variance stability. This approach reduces the risk of unstable estimates, misleading subgroup rankings, and unintended disclosure when working with highly disaggregated survey data.

A detailed explanation of the methodology is available in /docs/methodology.md.
